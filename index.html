Lost in Castle

A cinematic prototype built in Unreal.

Lost in Castle was my final semester project (Jan ‚Äì May 2025) ‚Äî a third-person action-adventure prototype built with Unreal Engine 5. I wanted to explore how camera work can shape how players feel, not just what they see.

Inspired by Silent Hill and Resident Evil, I built a system that blends fixed angles with spline-following cinematic cameras. Movement used classic tank controls, which ‚Äî surprisingly ‚Äî worked well with the dramatic camera shifts and added to the tension.

The prototype includes basic combat, enemy AI (via behavior trees), and interaction systems, all done through Blueprints. Even without full levels or smart enemies, it feels like a game that *could* exist.

Most of my focus was on getting the camera transitions to feel smooth ‚Äî blending from static shots to dynamic tracking paths using trigger volumes and splines. The system actually worked better than I expected ‚Äî but only when everything was planned down to the decimal.

I learned why this style fell out of favor: it demands absurd precision. Misplacing a trigger by a few units could break the camera logic or cause blind spots. You have to decide exactly what you want players to see, when, and how it should feel. That gave me a whole new respect for how effortlessly games like Silent Hill (1999) pulled it off.

It‚Äôs a lost art, honestly. I‚Äôd love to revisit this someday ‚Äî and actually build a full game that leans into this kind of cinematic control.

  • ‚Ä† Unreal Blueprints
  • ‚Ä† Cinematic camera scripting
  • ‚Ä† Tank controls & fixed-angle design
  • ‚Ä† Modular environments + VFX (Niagara)

MetaCam

Motion Capture Tool was my minor project for Semester 7 (Aug ‚Äì Nov 2024), where I tried building a DIY motion-capture pipeline using Python and open-source tools ‚Äî no fancy suits or studio setups.

It works by detecting pose, face, and hand landmarks in real-time using MediaPipe, then exporting that motion data as a timestamped JSON file that‚Äôs game-engine ready. The goal was to generate simple animations using just a webcam or phone camera.

I built a custom interface with Python and Pygame where users can switch between camera inputs, record motion clips, and watch live pose tracking as they move. While I didn‚Äôt get to test it directly inside Unreal Engine, the export logic is there ‚Äî the JSON is clean, structured, and ready to rig.

Most of the heavy lifting ‚Äî like pose estimation and JSON structuring ‚Äî was done by adapting open-source code with a *lot* of AI help (a little thing called vibe coding).

The whole idea was to make animation more accessible ‚Äî especially for solo devs or indies without expensive mocap gear. Honestly, I had zero idea how this worked before, but with a bit of trial, error, and AI assistance, I managed to ship a working prototype.

It‚Äôs rough around the edges, sure ‚Äî but it taught me a lot about data flow, pose estimation, and real-time input handling. I‚Äôd love to revisit this someday and take it one step further into actual skeletal animation.

HD_2D_Cams ‚Äî Camera Experiments in a 2D/3D World

Zoom in. Zoom out. Flip perspective. This prototype gave my HD_2D characters a camera system they could finally breathe in.

After the issues in my first HD_2D character test ‚Äî walls blocking the player, camera getting stuck, zero rotation ‚Äî I decided to rebuild the camera system from scratch. Not to give full modern control, but just enough flexibility to keep the player visible and the environment readable.

This prototype added zoom control (via up/down arrows) and a 3-angle toggle (via the C key) to flip between three fixed but distinct views ‚Äî straight-on, slightly angled, and almost top-down. These weren't true orbiting cameras, but simple swaps that made a big difference in visibility.

The biggest breakthrough was occlusion masking. Using a depth-based material trick (from Unreal‚Äôs own training video: ‚ÄúDepth & Location Based Effects‚Äù), I made any obstructing 3D walls or props fade out when they blocked the sprite. It wasn‚Äôt perfect, but suddenly the character was no longer lost behind crates or stairs.

All logic was done in Blueprints ‚Äî no plugins, no C++. Just trial, error, and slowly wiring together a more playable HD_2D camera system.

  • ‚Ä† Blueprint-based camera system
  • ‚Ä† Zoom control tied to input
  • ‚Ä† Fixed-angle camera toggle (3 views)
  • ‚Ä† Wall occlusion masking using material logic
  • ‚Ä† Built in Unreal Engine 5 with no external tools

Fire Material ‚Äî Stylized Torch Flame Inspired by Zelda

When Niagara melted my laptop, I had to rethink how fire even works.

This wasn‚Äôt just a torch effect ‚Äî it was a crash course in optimization, layering, and stylized art. I was working on my action-adventure project Lost in Castle, and my original fire system used Unreal‚Äôs Niagara particle system with thousands of particles per torch. It looked okay‚Ä¶ until I had a dozen torches. Frame rate dropped. Lighting washed out. Everything turned into a wall of glowing white.

So I scrapped it and rebuilt the fire from scratch using a layered shader approach. Inspired by Diablo III‚Äôs effects breakdown in the link given below (if any one's interested), I used texture blending and panning instead of brute force particles.

I hand-drew a stylized flame shape in Photoshop, then layered it with 2‚Äì3 scrolling noise textures to simulate motion and distortion. No flipbooks. No baked simulations. Just alpha-masked planes with clever math.

Once the base material was set, I applied it to a minimal Niagara setup ‚Äî around 10‚Äì20 particles max per torch. The result? More readable fire, stylized glow, and a dramatic cut in GPU load. It looked closer to Zelda: Breath of the Wild than I expected ‚Äî not exact, but on the right path.

This wasn‚Äôt just an effect ‚Äî it became a reminder that performance and style aren‚Äôt opposites. And it deserves its own slot, separate from the rest of the project that made it necessary.

  • ‚Ä† Stylized fire shader using layered scrolling textures
  • ‚Ä† Photoshop-created alpha masks and noise maps
  • ‚Ä† Niagara system with 90% fewer particles
  • ‚Ä† Inspired by Diablo III and Zelda fire techniques
  • ‚Ä† Built for performance-constrained systems
